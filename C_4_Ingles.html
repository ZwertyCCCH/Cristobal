📖 GRAMMAR PART 1

🔍 COMPARATIVES

We use comparatives to compare TWO things or people.

Short adjectives

adjective + -er + than

old → older
My brother is older than me.

tall → taller
Tom is taller than Sam.

Long adjectives

more + adjective + than

expensive → more expensive
Roller skates are more expensive than chocolates!

intelligent → more intelligent
She is more intelligent than her brother.

💡 Remember: one-syllable = add -er / two or more syllables = more + adj
🏆 GRAMMAR PART 2

⭐ SUPERLATIVES

We use superlatives to compare ONE person/thing to the whole group (the most, the best...).

Short adjectives

the + adjective + -est

I'm the oldest of my friends.

She is the tallest in the class.

Long adjectives

the most + adjective

He is the most intelligent 17-year-old in the school!

This is the most interesting photo.

⚡ Irregular forms: good → better → best / bad → worse → worst

🎯 ADVERBS OF DEGREE

📈 Make adjectives stronger or weaker

Adverbs of degree tell us HOW MUCH. They go BEFORE the adjective.

Stronger meaning

very / extremely

✔️ He's very shy.
✔️ She's extremely generous.

My best friend is very kind.

Weaker meaning

a bit / quite

✔️ I'm a bit tired.
✔️ He's quite funny.

Sometimes he's quite shy.

🔹 Order: adverb + adjective
Example: extremely beautiful / a bit nervous / very smart
✨ REAL EXAMPLES

📸 Describing friends & photos

Photo wall vs Computer:
"Photos on your wall are better than photos on your computer."
"It's cheaper to print in black and white, but colour looks more beautiful."
Personality adjectives + degree:
• She's really generous — she always buys presents.
• He's quite shy — only two close friends.
• My friend is extremely funny!
• I'm not very confident telling jokes.

Tip: Use "a bit" to make an adjective weaker, "very" to make it stronger.

🎯 ADVERBS OF DEGREE

📚 10 Common Adverbs of Degree

They modify adjectives or other adverbs → placed BEFORE the word they modify.

1. extremely — This test is extremely difficult.
2. very — My cat is very friendly.
3. quite — The movie was quite interesting.
4. a bit — I’m a bit tired today.
5. really — She is really kind to everyone.
6. almost — It’s almost impossible to win.
7. absolutely — That’s absolutely perfect!
8. completely — I’m completely sure about my answer.
9. too — The coffee is too hot to drink.
10. enough — She isn’t tall enough to reach. (goes AFTER adjective)
💡 Exception: "enough" goes AFTER the adjective (big enough, fast enough).
📝 LET'S REVIEW

✅ What did we learn?

  • Comparative → compare 2 things: older than, more expensive than
  • Superlative → compare to whole group: the oldest, the most intelligent
  • Adverbs of degree → before adjectives: very, quite, a bit, extremely
